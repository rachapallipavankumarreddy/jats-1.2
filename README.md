<abstract>
<p>In physics and chemistry, quantum systems are typically modeled using energy constraints formulated as Hamiltonians. Investigations into such systems often focus on the evolution of the Hamiltonians under various initial conditions, an approach summarized as <italic>Adiabatic Quantum Computing</italic> (<italic>AQC</italic>). Although this perspective may initially seem foreign to functional programmers, we demonstrate that conventional functional programming abstractions&#x2014;specifically, the <styled-content style="color:#CC4747"><monospace>Traversable</monospace></styled-content> and <styled-content style="color:#CC4747"><monospace>Monad</monospace></styled-content>  type classes&#x2014;naturally capture the essence of <italic>AQC</italic>. To illustrate this connection, we introduce <sc>EnQ</sc>, a functional programming library designed to express diverse optimization problems as energy constraint computations (<italic>ECC</italic>). The library comprises three core components: generating the solution space, associating energy costs with potential solutions, and searching for optimal or near-optimal solutions. Because <sc>EnQ</sc> is implemented using standard Haskell, it can be executed directly through conventional classical Haskell compilers. More interestingly, we develop and implement a process to compile <sc>EnQ</sc> programs into circuits executable on quantum hardware. We validate <sc>EnQ</sc>&#x2019;s effectiveness through a number of case studies, demonstrating its capacity to express and solve classical optimization problems on quantum hardware, including search problems, type inference, number partitioning, clique finding, and graph coloring.</p>

<sec id="sec1">
<label>1</label>
<title>Introduction</title>
<p>Quantum computing has achieved significant milestones in recent years, enabling solutions to computational problems that remain intractable for classical computers [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">Bravyi et al. 2024</xref>;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17"> King et al. 2024</xref>]. A well-known example is Shor&#x2019;s algorithm [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib90">Shor 1994</xref>], which factors integers in polynomial time&#x2013;an ability not known to be feasible in the classical setting. Despite these advancements, quantum programming remains highly challenging due to the complexity of existing quantum programming languages [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib8">Amy et al. 2017</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib12">Beauregard 2003</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib27">Daley et al. 2022</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib36">Fagan and Duncan 2018</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib49">H&#x00E4;ner et al. 2017</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib50">Hietala et al. 2023</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib54">Ittah et al. 2021</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib49"> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib67">Li et al. 2022</xref></xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib70">Litteken et al. 2020</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib74">Markov and Saeedi 2012</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib77">Nam et al. 2018</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib80">Peng et al. 2024</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib85">Rigetti Computing 2019</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib101">Xu et al. 2022</xref>], i.e., many of these languages expose too many low-level circuit details, requiring programmers to manage intricate quantum machine constraints&#x2013;akin to writing in assembly language with bit manipulations.</p>
<p>To unlock the full potential of quantum computing, we need higher-level abstractions that allow developers to focus on problem-solving rather than machine-level implementation details.</p>
<p>A key challenge in quantum programming is the disconnect between the reality of quantum hardware and the abstractions provided by programming languages. Current quantum computers operate as particle system simulators, where computations are defined through Hamiltonian matrices&#x2013;mathematical representations of system energy&#x2013;evolved over time. In practice, every quantum gate operation is simulated via a user-defined Hamiltonian that evolves over a set period, creating an implicit yet critical compilation layer beneath conventional gate-based programming.</p>
<p>This gap between programming models and quantum hardware introduces two major issues. First, quantum computing is already constrained by hardware limitations, and the need for explicit gate simulation further amplifies inefficiencies. Second, usability remains a concern. Besides a few applications based on digital, circuit-based quantum languages, such as Shor&#x2019;s algorithm for breaking encryptions, a large group of scientific researchers often prefer to model problems directly as Hamiltonian systems [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib19">Cao et al. 2019</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib32">Du and Vary 2023</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib34">Emani et al. 2021</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib35">Evangelista and Batista 2023</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib39">Fedorov and Gelfand 2021</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib64">Kreshchuk et al. 2021</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib65">Lanyon et al. 2010</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib75">McArdle et al. 2020</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib79">Outeiral et al. 2021</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib104">Zhang et al. 2021</xref>], with compilation bypassing digital circuit languages altogether.</p>
<p>One established quantum computational model, adiabatic quantum computing (AQC), naturally aligns with this paradigm. AQC formulates constraint problems as Hamiltonian simulations, evolving an initial quantum state over time to reach a low-energy (optimal) configuration. While AQC provides a theoretical foundation for solving optimization problems, it lacks a well-defined programming model for constructing Hamiltonians efficiently. Existing classical approaches, such as Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO), enable NP-hard problems to be mapped to physical Ising models, but these formulations remain low-level, requiring manual manipulation of qubits and offering only limited quantum computational power.</p>
<p>Our first observation is that many Hamiltonians corresponding to scientific and engineering problems of interest can be framed as constraint-satisfaction problems, which are traditionally solved using a generate-and-test approach. This method systematically generates potential solutions, evaluates them based on constraints, and searches for optimal candidates. In functional programming languages like Haskell, this paradigm can be elegantly expressed through monadic traversals. For example, we can express the problem of partitioning a list into two sublists of equal sum as follows:</p>

<p>Summing and squaring the numbers in each sublist produces [36,4,0,16,16,0,4,36] where the occurrences of 0 are associated with the perfect partitions [1,-3,2] and [-1,3,-2]. <p>While this classical approach is well-understood and effective, we demonstrate that the same high-level abstractions can be compiled to quantum hardware to leverage the potential advantages of quantum computing. Specifically, we replace the classical implementation of <styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>choices</monospace></styled-content> with a traversal that associates with each list element a native quantum superposition (i.e., a qubit) algebraically modeled by a  &#x1D44D;  operator with  &#x00B1;1 eigenvalues. In this case, we will have three  &#x1D44D;  operators  &#x1D44D;(0),  &#x1D44D;(1), and  &#x1D44D;(2) where  &#x1D44D;(<italic>i</italic>) is associated with the list element at index <italic>i</italic>. The classical energy function is replaced by so-called  &#x1D44D;&#x1D44D;  quantum correlations specifying the cost of aligning the individual choices. As explained in the main body of the paper, this process generates the following correlations 6 &#x00B7;  &#x1D44D;(0)&#x1D44D;(1) +4 &#x00B7;  &#x1D44D;(0) &#x1D44D;(2) + 12 &#x00B7;  &#x1D44D;(1)&#x1D44D;(2) indicating that the cost of aligning  &#x1D44D;(1) with  &#x1D44D;(0) or  &#x1D44D;(2) is much higher than the cost of aligning  &#x1D44D;(0) with  &#x1D44D;(2). As physical systems naturally prefer to stabilize in low energy configurations, the quantum system will, with high probability, prefer to align  &#x1D44D;(0) and  &#x1D44D;(2), yielding the perfect solution to the original partition problem.</p>
<p>Our technical contributions can be summarized as follows. (See <xref ref-type="fig" rid="f_1.">Fig. 1.</xref>)</p>
<list list-type="bullet">
<list-item><p>Design and Implementation: We develop an embedded domain-specific language (DSL) that generalizes functional constraint-solving paradigms for quantum programming.</p></list-item>
<list-item><p>Expressiveness: We demonstrate the versatility of our DSL through a diverse set of optimiza&#x2010;tion problems, including search, type inference, clique finding, and graph coloring.</p></list-item>
<list-item><p>Quantum Compilation: We construct a compiler targeting multiple quantum backends, in&#x2010;cluding circuit-based quantum computers and Hamiltonian-based adiabatic simulators.</p></list-item>
<list-item><p>Experimental Validation: We present results from both classical simulations and executions on IBM quantum processors, showcasing the effectiveness of our approach.</p></list-item>
</list>

However, many multi-qubit values cannot be separated and expressed as the tensor product of smaller values; such inseparable value states are called <italic>entangled</italic>, e.g. <inline-formula> <mml:math display="inline"> <mml:mrow> <mml:mfrac> <mml:mn>1</mml:mn> <mml:mrow> <mml:msqrt> <mml:mn>2</mml:mn> </mml:msqrt> </mml:mrow> </mml:mfrac> <mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mrow><mml:mo>|</mml:mo><mml:mrow> <mml:mn>00</mml:mn> </mml:mrow><mml:mo>&#x27E9;</mml:mo></mml:mrow><mml:mo>+</mml:mo><mml:mrow><mml:mo>|</mml:mo><mml:mrow> <mml:mn>11</mml:mn> </mml:mrow><mml:mo>&#x27E9;</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:mrow><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:mrow> </mml:math> </inline-formula>, known as a <italic>Bell pair</italic>, which can be rewritten to  <inline-formula> <mml:math display="inline"> <mml:mrow> <mml:msubsup> <mml:mo>&#x2211;</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mi>d</mml:mi><mml:mo>=</mml:mo><mml:mn>0</mml:mn> </mml:mrow> <mml:mn>1</mml:mn> </mml:msubsup> <mml:mrow> <mml:mfrac> <mml:mn>1</mml:mn> <mml:mrow> <mml:msqrt> <mml:mn>2</mml:mn> </mml:msqrt> </mml:mrow> </mml:mfrac> <mml:mrow><mml:mo>|</mml:mo><mml:mrow> <mml:mi>d</mml:mi><mml:mi>d</mml:mi> </mml:mrow><mml:mo>&#x27E9;</mml:mo></mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> </mml:math> </inline-formula>, where &#x1D451;&#x1D451; is a bit string consisting of two bits, each of which must be the same value (i.e., &#x1D451; = 0 or &#x1D451; = 1). Each term <inline-formula> <mml:math display="inline"> <mml:mrow> <mml:mfrac> <mml:mn>1</mml:mn> <mml:mrow> <mml:msqrt> <mml:mn>2</mml:mn> </mml:msqrt> </mml:mrow> </mml:mfrac> <mml:mrow><mml:mo>|</mml:mo><mml:mrow> <mml:mi>d</mml:mi><mml:mi>d</mml:mi> </mml:mrow><mml:mo>&#x27E9;</mml:mo></mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> </mml:math> </inline-formula> is named a <italic>basis-ket</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib78">Nielsen and Chuang 2011</xref>], consisting an amplitude <inline-formula> <mml:math display="inline"> <mml:mrow> <mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mfrac> <mml:mn>1</mml:mn> <mml:mrow> <mml:msqrt> <mml:mn>2</mml:mn> </mml:msqrt> </mml:mrow> </mml:mfrac></mml:mrow> <mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> </mml:math></inline-formula>  and a basis vector <inline-formula> <mml:math display="inline"> <mml:mrow> <mml:mrow><mml:mo>|</mml:mo><mml:mrow> <mml:mi>d</mml:mi><mml:mi>d</mml:mi> </mml:mrow><mml:mo>&#x27E9;</mml:mo></mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> </mml:math> </inline-formula>. </p>
<p><italic><bold>Circuit-based Quantum Computation and Operations.</bold></italic> Computation on a quantum value consists of a series of quantum operations, each acting on a subset of qubits in the quantum state. A quantum circuit model description expresses quantum computations as <italic>circuits</italic>. In these circuits, each horizontal wire represents a qubit, and boxes on these wires indicate quantum operations, or gates. This evolution is mathematically expressed by multiplying the state vector by the gate&#x2019;s corresponding matrix representation; single-qubit gates are 2-by-2 matrices, and two-qubit gates are 4-by-4 matrices. The matrix representation of a gate must be <italic>unitary</italic>, ensuring that it preserves the norm of the quantum state&#x2019;s amplitudes. An entire quantum circuit can be described by composing its constituent gates into a single unitary matrix. This matrix representation encapsulates the overall transformation applied to the initial quantum state.</p>
<p><italic><bold>Energy Constraint Computation (ECC). </bold></italic>A Hamiltonian is an operator that represents the total energy of a system. In an ECC, we reduce arbitrary optimization problems to the problem of optimizing a corresponding Hamiltonian, i.e., to minimizing energy. One of the classical ECC schemes is QUBO, a combinatorial optimization problem with various applications from finance and economics to machine learning. QUBO is an NP-hard problem, and for many classical problems from theoretical computer science, like maximum cut, graph coloring, and the partition problem, embeddings into QUBO have been formulated. Embeddings for machine learning models include support-vector machines, clustering, and probabilistic graphical models. Moreover, due to its close connection to Ising models, QUBO constitutes a central problem class for adiabatic quantum computation, where it is solved through a physical process called quantum annealing.</p>
<p><italic><bold>Hamiltonian Simulation and Adiabatic Quantum Computation.</bold></italic> AQC is a model of quan&#x2010;tum computation that solves optimization problems by evolving a quantum system slowly from an initial Hamiltonian <italic>H<sub>i</sub></italic> with a known ground state to a final Hamiltonian <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> whose ground state encodes the solution. By maintaining the system in its lowest energy state throughout the process, AQC leverages the adiabatic theorem to find optimal solutions efficiently. The first line below shows the Hamiltonian <italic>H</italic> that varies with time <italic>t</italic> from <italic>H<sub>i</sub></italic> to <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic>. The exact evolution of this Hamiltonian would be given by the Schr&#x00F6;dinger equation on the right. The actual solution used in AQC is a Hamiltonian simulation consisting of the sequence on the second line where each step generates a quantum operation via a matrix exponential operation (exp(-<italic>iH</italic>(<italic>t</italic>) &#x0394;<italic>t</italic>)) for a small time period &#x0394;<italic>t</italic>. Note that a Hamiltonian is a Hermitian matrix (Hermitian <italic>H</italic> = <italic>H</italic><sup>&#x2020;</sup>) and that Hamiltonian simulation (exp(-<italic>iHt</italic>)) generates a unitary matrix that can be implemented as a quantum circuit. Therefore, the AQC procedure can be understood as starting with an initial quantum state |&#x1D711;(0)&#x27E9; and applying a sequence of unitary quantum operations, each of which is of the form exp(-<italic>iH</italic>(<italic>t</italic>) &#x0394;<italic>t</italic>) for a different <italic>t</italic> &#x2208; &#x007B;&#x0394;<italic>t</italic>, 2&#x0394;<italic>t</italic>, 3&#x0394;<italic>t</italic>,..., <italic>T</italic>&#x007D;.</p>

<title>Energy Constrained Computation</title>
<p>We introduce the Ising formalism in which ECC problems are typically formulated by physicists, but using low-level computational abstractions. <sc>EnQ</sc> lifts the Ising formalism to a more general setting.</p>
<sec id="sec3_1">
<label>3.1</label>
<title>The Original Energy Constrained Computation</title>
<p>The Ising model [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Barahona 1982;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib20">Chakrabarti 1981</xref>] represents quantum systems using a collection of particles situated on a grid; each particle influences its neighbors using specified forces, and the goal is often to find a stable low-energy configuration. This model can be used to elegantly express classical optimization problems [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib20">Chakrabarti 1981</xref>], including all the textbook NP-hard problems [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib72">Lucas 2014a</xref>]. Here, we review three examples: equal-sum, &#x1D43E;-clique finding, and graph coloring.</p>
<p>The equal-sum problem introduced in the introduction can be formalized as follows. We consider a one-dimensional lattice where each list element <italic>&#x1D463;<sub>&#x1D62A;</sub></italic> is positioned at a discrete grid point and assigned a spin <italic>s</italic><sub><italic>i</italic></sub> &#x2208; &#x007B;&#x00B1;1&#x007D;. A more involved example is to decide if a graph <italic>G</italic> = (<italic>V</italic>, <italic>E</italic>) has a <italic>K</italic>-clique. <sec id="sec3_2">
<label>3.2</label>
<title>Towards High-Level Abstractions</title>
<p>The above approach to solving optimization problems can be summarized in three steps: (i) writing down an energy cost function, (ii) producing all the possible solutions based on the cost function, and (iii) finding the minimal solution. Although the basic idea is simple and elegant, its realization is quite tedious. In particular:</p>
<list list-type="bullet">
<list-item><p>Each example above naturally refers to an abstract data structure, e.g., a list or a graph. By using array indices to access the components of the underlying data structure, this approach essentially forces a low-level encoding of every data structure as an array.</p></list-item>
<list-item><p>In the presentation above, choices and energies are conflated. In one example, we represent choices using &#x007B;&#x00B1;1&#x007D; and in another we represent them as  &#x007B;0, 1&#x007D;. In both cases, we have a binary choice, but the representation is tailored to match the definition of energy. Ideally, the choices should be an abstract type, e.g., <styled-content style="color:#CC4747"><monospace>Color</monospace></styled-content>, with no initial connection to energy. The definition of energy may later map these abstract choices to particular values depending on the problem.</p></list-item>
<list-item><p>In the <italic>K</italic>-clique example, the term (<italic>K</italic> &#x2212; &#x2211;<sub><italic>j</italic></sub><italic>x</italic><sub><italic>j</italic></sub>)<sup>2</sup> is needed to constrain the explored configurations to subgraphs with exactly <italic>K</italic> marked elements. These are the only valid choices that might lead to a solution. The traversal of the graph using array indices is, however, forced to explore all configurations, including a large number of invalid ones.</p></list-item>
<list-item><p>Finally, the specification of Hamiltonians above is committed to using classical choices. The move to a quantum representation of choices might be direct, like in the equal-sum example, but generally, the Hamiltonians for the quantum adiabatic computation simulation might need to be redesigned in substantial ways [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib5">Albash and Lidar 2018</xref>].</p></list-item>
</list>
</sec>

<sec id="sec4_2">
<label>4.2</label>
<title>Common Semantics Infrastructure</title>
<p>We provide two execution pathways to solve the optimization problem specified by the <styled-content style="color:#A31545"><monospace>Program</monospace></styled-content> specification: a classical one and a quantum one. A standalone classical implementation would be fairly direct, as illustrated by the solution to the equal sum problem in the introduction. However, one of our aims is to share as much infrastructure as possible between the classical and quantum pathways; our approach isolates the truly quantum-dependent aspects into just one component. In more detail, we will decompose the implementations into four stages: <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>genChoices</monospace></styled-content>, <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>distinctNTuples</monospace></styled-content>, <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>assignChoices</monospace></styled-content>, and <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>solve</monospace></styled-content>. The first three steps are shared by the classical and quantum interpretations. The only difference is the <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>solve</monospace></styled-content> step.</p>

<title>The EnQ Quantum Interpretation</title>
<p>Building on the classical workflow introduced in <xref ref-type="sec" rid="sec4">Section 4</xref>, where we enumerated every choice and explicitly computed its energy, we now explain how the same optimization problems are executed on quantum hardware in <sc>EnQ</sc>. At a high level, the quantum path retains the three conceptual stages of the classical one as follows.</p>

<list-item><p>Prepare a superposition that contains all candidate solutions.</p></list-item>
<list-item><p>Translate the classical cost function into a quantum Hamiltonian whose ground state encodes the optimum.</p></list-item>
<list-item><p>Guide the system toward that ground state, either by a gate-based AQC or, where appropriate, by a restricted version of quantum annealing, a.k.a., the D-Wave annealer.</p></list-item>
</list>
<p>Concretely, <sc>EnQ</sc> offers two complementary quantum backends. The first backend compiles the AQC process using a sequence of <inline-formula><mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>exp(</mml:mi><mml:mo>-</mml:mo><mml:mi>&#x1D456;</mml:mi><mml:mi>&#x1D43B;</mml:mi><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:mi>&#x1D460;</mml:mi><mml:mo>)</mml:mo><mml:mo>&#x0394;</mml:mo><mml:mi>&#x1D461;</mml:mi><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math></inline-formula> applications as explained in <xref ref-type="sec" rid="sec2">Section 2</xref>. These intermediate results are then mapped to conventional quantum digital circuits that can run on gate-model devices such as those provided by IBM Qiskit. The second backend targets D-Wave&#x2019;s quantum-annealing platform, mapping suitable problems directly onto its native Ising Hamiltonian and thereby sidestepping circuit compilation altogether. Throughout this section, we highlight the parallels between these quantum executions and their classical counterparts from <xref ref-type="sec" rid="sec4">Section 4</xref>, so that the reader can see how each quantum construct arises naturally from the earlier classical description.</p>
<sec id="sec5_1">
<label>5.1</label>
<title>Generating the Quantum Solution Space (Superposition of Choices)</title>
<p>The classical implementation explicitly traverses all possible choices to build the solution space. For instance, a list of &#x1D45B; variables with two options each would generate <inline-formula><mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>N</mml:mi><mml:mo>=</mml:mo><mml:msup><mml:mn>2</mml:mn><mml:mi>n</mml:mi></mml:msup></mml:mrow></mml:math></inline-formula> combinations. Instead of iterating through each combination one by one, we prepare a quantum superposition that represents all &#x1D441; possible solutions simultaneously. We allocate a set of qubits to encode the choice variables (e.g., one qubit per binary choice).</p>

<p>There might be different initial superposition states. We provide two examples here. A typical example of an initial quantum state is a uniform superposition state. The state has <inline-formula><italic>&#x0032;</italic><sup><italic>&#x1D45B;</italic></sup></inline-formula> different basis-kets, each with amplitude being <inline-formula> <mml:math display="inline"> <mml:mrow> <mml:mfrac> <mml:mn>1</mml:mn> <mml:msqrt> <mml:msup> <mml:mn>2</mml:mn> <mml:mi>n</mml:mi> </mml:msup> </mml:msqrt> </mml:mfrac> </mml:mrow> </mml:math> </inline-formula> and a basis-vector <inline-formula><italic>&#x007C;</italic><italic>&#x1D457;</italic><italic>&#x27E9;</italic></inline-formula>. In the quantum setting of the clique finding example, we want to start with an <italic>&#x1D45B;</italic> qubit initial superposition state of &#x1D43E;-th hamming weights, <inline-formula> <mml:math display="inline"> <mml:mrow> <mml:msup> <mml:mrow> <mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mfrac linethickness="0"> <mml:mi>n</mml:mi> <mml:mi>K</mml:mi> </mml:mfrac> <mml:mo>)</mml:mo> </mml:mrow> <mml:mrow> <mml:mo>-</mml:mo> <mml:mfrac> <mml:mn>1</mml:mn> <mml:mn>2</mml:mn> </mml:mfrac> </mml:mrow> </mml:msup> <mml:munderover> <mml:mo>&#x2211;</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mtext>h</mml:mtext> <mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mi>j</mml:mi> <mml:mo>)</mml:mo> <mml:mo>=</mml:mo> <mml:mi>K</mml:mi> </mml:mrow> <mml:none/> </mml:munderover> <mml:mrow> <mml:mo>|</mml:mo> <mml:mi>j</mml:mi> <mml:mo>&#x27E9;</mml:mo> </mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> </mml:math> </inline-formula>, where &#x210E;(&#x1D457;) checks the bitstring representation of &#x1D457; having exactly &#x1D43E; copies of 1. <p><bold><italic>Initial Hamiltonians and <sc>EnQ</sc> Encoding.</italic></bold> As mentioned in <xref ref-type="sec" rid="sec2">Section 2</xref>, in the general AQC procedure, each initial state is associated with an initial Hamiltonian, where the initial state is an eigenstate (ground state) of the initial Hamiltonian. Intuitively, &#x1D43B;<sub>&#x1D456;</sub> does not encode any problem constraints; it is chosen to make it easy for the quantum system to start in its ground state.</p>
<p>Depending on the initial state, the initial Hamiltonian is chosen differently. A common choice (and the one used in <sc>EnQ</sc>) is a mixing Hamiltonian that flips qubits between 0 and 1 states. For example, this can be written as a sum of Pauli-&#x1D44B; operations on each qubit (recall that &#x1D44B; |0&#x27E9; =|1&#x27E9; and &#x1D44B; |1&#x27E9; = |0&#x27E9;) for the above uniform superposition state. In our formulation, we denote a similar operation using creation/annihilation operators &#x1D44E;&#x2020; and &#x1D44E; (in the so-called second quantization formalism in <xref ref-type="sec" rid="sec2">Section 2</xref>): one can write <inline-formula> <mml:math display='inline'> <mml:mrow> <mml:msub> <mml:mi>H</mml:mi> <mml:mi>i</mml:mi> </mml:msub> <mml:mo>=</mml:mo><mml:msub> <mml:mo>&#x2211;</mml:mo> <mml:mi>k</mml:mi> </mml:msub> <mml:mrow> <mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mi>a</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mi>k</mml:mi> <mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow> <mml:mo>+</mml:mo><mml:msup> <mml:mi>a</mml:mi> <mml:mo>&#x2020;</mml:mo> </mml:msup> <mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mi>k</mml:mi> <mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> <mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> </mml:math> </inline-formula>, which is mathematically analogous to &#x2211;<sub><italic>&#x1D458;</italic></sub> &#x0058;<sub><italic>&#x1D458;</italic></sub> and has the uniform superposition as an eigenstate. By preparing the qubits in the |0&#x27E9;state (the ground state of &#x1D43B;<italic>&#x1D456;</italic>), we have essentially initialized the quantum system with all potential solutions represented, analogous to how the classical algorithm initialized a data structure to hold all combinations.</p>

<p>The initial Hamiltonian &#x1D43B;<sub><italic>&#x1D456;</italic></sub> can be set to the above one. A single term, <inline-formula id="IEQ3"><italic>&#x0061;</italic><sup><italic>&#x2020;</italic></sup>&#x0028;<italic>&#x1D457;</italic>&#x0029; <italic>&#x0061;</italic>&#x0028;<italic>&#x1D458;</italic>&#x0029; &#x002B; <italic>&#x0061;</italic>&#x0028;<italic>&#x1D457;</italic>&#x0029; <italic>&#x0061;</italic><sup>&#x2020;</sup>&#x0028;<italic>&#x1D458;</italic>&#x0029;<italic>&#x002C;</italic></inline-formula> has the effect that if the &#x1D457;-th and &#x1D458;-th qubits have the composed vector state |0&#x27E9; |1&#x27E9; or |1&#x27E9; |0&#x27E9; in two different basis-kets, we then swap the two in the assembly line; if they have the vector states |0&#x27E9; |0&#x27E9; or |1&#x27E9; |1&#x27E9;, we then remove the two basis-kets from the whole quantum state. <p>The <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>initialStates</monospace></styled-content> function (<xref ref-type="fig" rid="f_5">Figure 5</xref>) compiles the data-structure choices to an initial superposition state by generating the quantum circuits. For the case where <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>length</monospace></styled-content> cs is not a power of 2, one can always insert unused choices to make the choice number a power of 2, and there is an additional compilation step to permit the AQC process to avoid selecting the unused choices; details are in  <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">Aharonov et al. <p><bold><italic>Heuristics for Initial Hamiltonians.</italic></bold> The proper selection of &#x1D43B;<sub><italic>&#x1D456;</italic></sub> might affect the convergence time of the AQC system; however, based on the <sc>EnQ</sc> programmer&#x2019;s point of view, the &#x1D43B;<sub><italic>&#x1D456;</italic></sub> selection is related to the machinery properties of AQC and the data-structure choices, rather than a necessary program component. This paper assumes that the initial Hamiltonian generation is correctly generated in our backend based on heuristics, and programmers using our API do not need to program the initial Hamiltonians. When compiling an <sc>EnQ</sc> program to the AQC process, we assume that the correct initial Hamiltonian &#x1D43B;<sub><italic>&#x1D456;</italic></sub> is based on the user-defined initial superposition states.</p>

<sec id="sec5_2">
<label>5.2</label>
<title>Encoding Energy Association as a Quantum Hamiltonian</title>
<p>In the classical DSL, once the solution space is generated, we compute an energy cost or score for each candidate solution using the user-defined constraints. For example, if our problem is a graph clique finder, the classical code evaluates each subset of vertices and assigns a penalty for each pair of chosen vertices that are not connected. The best solution minimizes these penalties. In the quantum setting, we translate the classical cost function into a problem Hamiltonian <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic>, a quantum operator (energy function) acting on the qubits, such that each basis state&#x2019;s energy equals the classical cost for that solution. In other words, <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> is constructed so that its lowest-energy eigenstate corresponds to the optimal (minimum-cost) solution of the original problem. This key step links the abstract problem definition to a physical energy landscape that the quantum system will naturally try to minimize.</p>
<p>To build <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic>, we consider each constraint or energy term from the classical program and create a corresponding quantum operator. Each term in <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> is designed to penalize undesired configurations of qubit states, like the classical constraint penalizes specific variable assignments. We accomplish this by examining a constraint&#x2019;s relevant qubits (locations) and assigning an operator that gives a positive energy contribution if and only if the constraint is violated (or subtracts energy when the constraint is satisfied). By summing all such contributions, the total Hamiltonian <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> will have lower energy for better-fitting solutions and higher energy for worse solutions.</p>
<p>In practice, <sc>EnQ</sc> represents boolean choices on qubits in a convenient way for encoding constraints. We examine the <sc>EnQ</sc> setting to see the logic behind the constraint encoding. In <sc>EnQ</sc>, the code after <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace> initialStates</monospace></styled-content> performs energy association to link data structure choices with energy scores. In the <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>solve</monospace></styled-content> function in <xref ref-type="fig" rid="fig4">Figure 4</xref>, we assign choices to a data structure pattern. We assign energy scores by setting up viewpoints for locating sub-regions of the data structure pattern.</p> 
<p>In constructing a Hamiltonian, users typically manipulate a location with the operation form as <inline-formula id="IEQ3.1"> <mml:math display="inline"> <mml:mrow> <mml:mi>&#x1D6FC;</mml:mi> <mml:mo>&#x2217;</mml:mo> <mml:mi>op</mml:mi> <mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mi>x</mml:mi> <mml:mo>)</mml:mo> </mml:mrow> </mml:math> </inline-formula>, i.e., we apply an operation <italic>op</italic> to the (qubit) location <italic>x</italic>, with an amplitude adjustment &#x1D6FC;. For example, if a single qubit state is <inline-formula> <italic>&#x03B1;</italic><sub>j</sub> <italic> | b</italic><sub>j</sub><italic>&#x27E9;</italic> </inline-formula> in location <italic>x</italic>, the above operation might result in <inline-formula> <italic>(</italic> <italic>&#x03B1; &#x2217; &#x03B1;</italic><sub>j</sub> <italic>) | op</italic>(<italic>b</italic><sub>j</sub>)&#x27E9; </inline-formula>. The key to linking the problem Hamiltonian <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> and our energy association patterns is to examine the bits in the basis-ket state representing our location variables. For example, in a basis-ket <inline-formula>&#x03B1; |101&#x27E9;</inline-formula>, if we know that location <italic>x</italic> and <italic>y</italic> refer to the first and second qubits, we can restrict the view to be |10&#x27E9;, pattern match the ket state |10&#x27E9; with the energy patterns, and apply the corresponding energy score matching the pattern to the amplitude &#x1D6FC;. However, the above scheme does not work for the quantum setting since it requires the impossible step to observe the ket state in a quantum state.</p>
<p>This means we must find an operation <italic>op</italic>(<italic>x</italic>) to stabilize a ket state. For example, if we want to pattern match a |10&#x27E9; state, we need to find an operation <italic>op</italic> such that <italic>op</italic> |10&#x27E9; = |10&#x27E9; and <inline-formula><italic>op</italic>|b<sub>1</sub>b<sub>2</sub>&#x27E9; = <italic>O</italic></inline-formula> when <italic>b<sub>1</sub>b<sub>2</sub></italic> &#x2260; 10. Here, <italic>O</italic> is the zero vector. For a basis-ket having the form &#x23B8;<italic>b<sub>1</sub>,...,b<sub>n</sub></italic>&#x27E9;, if we are applying <italic>op</italic> to <italic>b<sub>j</sub>b<sub>k</sub></italic> and resulting in <italic>O</italic>, <italic>op</italic> essentially eliminates the basis-ket from the whole quantum state. If such an operation<italic>op</italic> exists, we can encode an energy constraint as the desired <italic>op</italic> that can quantumly select the pattern and then assign the energy score as the amplitude term of the operation; e.g., if <italic>op</italic> [10] stabilizes the |10&#x27E9; state and the energy score associate with [1,0] is 3, we can encode the energy constraint as an operation 3&#x2217;<italic>op</italic> [10]. In this case, we can translate all the choice patterns generated in the energy association step to this kind of operation and then generate a Hamiltonian by linearly summing all of the operations.</p>
<p>After assembling all constraint-derived terms, we obtain the whole problem Hamiltonian <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic>. This Hamiltonian encapsulates the original optimization problem: any eigenstate of <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> corresponds to some candidate solution, and its eigenvalue is the corresponding cost. By construction, the ground state of <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> is the optimal solution, and low-lying excited states correspond to near-optimal solutions. At this point, we have mapped the entire high-level problem into the quantum domain: the state space of our qubits represents the search space, and the Hamiltonian <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> represents the objective function.</p>
<p>The above task is handled by the <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>compiles</monospace></styled-content> function in <xref ref-type="fig" rid="f_5">Figure 5</xref>. The <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>decode</monospace></styled-content> step generates possible operations depending on the <monospace>choices</monospace> being selected, and the <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>conditions</monospace></styled-content> function creates choice and constraint patterns. The <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>compiles</monospace></styled-content> functions link the two steps by pairing an operation with the particular choice pattern it represents and then assigning the energy score associated with the choice pattern to the operation by multiplying the scores and the operation together. Eventually, we produce a circuit to create the initial state (<styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>initialStates</monospace></styled-content>) and the problem Hamiltonian (<styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>compiles</monospace></styled-content>), and we send it to the quantum computer, <styled-content style="color:#0000FF"><monospace>qiskit</monospace></styled-content>, for execution.</p>
<p>Notice that up to now, we have not &#x201C;found&#x201D; the answer; we have simply set up a quantum system that encodes the problem. Classically, one would now iterate through computed scores to pick the minimum or apply an algorithm to identify the best solution. In the quantum setting, this role is taken over by the adiabatic algorithm: the system evolves under a gradually changing Hamiltonian that steers it toward the lowest-energy state of <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic>, effectively performing the optimization step. For problems expressed directly as Ising Hamiltonians, this same process can be carried out via quantum annealing, where the system physically relaxes into a low-energy configuration, often bypassing the need for explicit circuit compilation.</p>
<p>The above encoding assumes the existence of an operation <italic>op</italic> with the kind of functionality. We will examine the operations in the next section when discussing quantum compilation.</p>
</sec>
<sec id="sec5_3">
<label>5.3</label>
<title>Adiabatic Evolution: From Initial State to Optimal Solution</title>
<p>Once we have the initial Hamiltonian <italic>H<sub>i</sub></italic> (easy to solve, with known ground state) and the final problem Hamiltonian <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> (whose ground state encodes the answer), we leverage AQC to find the optimum. The principle of AQC is to slowly interpolate between <italic>H<sub>i</sub></italic> and <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> over time, adiabatically guiding the quantum state from the known easy ground state to the unknown solution state. <xref ref-type="sec" rid="sec2">Section 2</xref> introduces AQC, and we examine the intuition of performing AQC for <sc>EnQ</sc> programs.</p>
<p>We conceptually define a time-dependent Hamiltonian <italic>H</italic>(<italic>s</italic>) that evolves from <italic>H</italic>(0) = <italic>H<sub>i</sub></italic> to <italic>H</italic>(1) = <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic>  as a function of a normalized time parameter <italic>s</italic> &#x2208; [0,1]. For example, a linear schedule would use <inline-formula> <italic>H</italic>(<italic>s</italic>) = (1 &#x2212; <italic>s</italic>)<italic>H<sub>i</sub></italic> + <italic>s</italic><italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> </inline-formula>. Initially (<italic>s</italic> = 0), the system is in the ground state of <italic>H<sub>i</sub></italic> (the uniform superposition). As <italic>s</italic> increases, the Hamiltonian gradually incorporates the problem constraints. If the change is slow enough, the quantum adiabatic theorem guarantees that the state of the system will remain in the instantaneous ground state of <italic>H</italic>(<italic>s</italic>). By the end (<italic>s</italic> = 1), the system will be in the ground state of <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic>, which is exactly the state encoding the optimal solution of our problem.</p>
<p>In practice, this continuous adiabatic evolution is realized through a sequence of many small discrete steps. Each step applies a slight alteration to the Hamiltonian and consequently a quantum operation on the state. Eventually, after the full schedule, the system&#x2019;s state ideally encodes the solution in its qubit amplitudes. Finally, we measure the qubits in the computational basis. With high probability (if the adiabatic process succeeded and if decoherence and other errors are minimal), the measurement will collapse the system into the basis state corresponding to the lowest energy, i.e., the optimal solution.</p>
<p>Adiabatic evolution replaces the explicit search or brute-force classical process with a physical process. As the Hamiltonian changes, the quantum state naturally &#x201C;searches&#x201D; the landscape of possibilities by continuously adjusting the amplitudes of basis states, concentrating more weight on low-energy (better) solutions. By the end, the amplitude is ideally concentrated on the optimal solution. This is analogous to a landscape where the system begins as a liquid spread out over all valleys and then slowly freezes into the lowest valley (the global minimum) due to the energy bias introduced by <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> . The power of this approach is that it exploits quantum parallelism and tunneling: the state can explore many solutions concurrently and avoid getting stuck in local minima under the right conditions, potentially outperforming certain classical algorithms on hard problems.</p>
</sec>
<sec id="sec5_4">
<label>5.4</label>
<title>Compiling the Adiabatic Process to Quantum Circuits</title>
<p>The description above of the adiabatic process is hardware-independent and assumes an ideal, continuously changing Hamiltonian. In practice, to run on real quantum hardware (e.g., gate-based quantum computers), we must compile this high-level procedure into a discrete sequence of quantum gates. <sc>EnQ</sc> automates this compilation, turning the initial state preparation and Hamiltonian evolution into a concrete circuit (for simulators or actual devices). We outline the key steps in this compilation process and the corresponding components in our Haskell implementation.</p>
<sec id="sec5_4_1">
<label>5.4.1</label>
<title><italic>From <sc>EnQ</sc> Program to Hamiltonian Terms.</italic></title>
<p>The first compilation step is to translate the abstract Hamiltonian <italic>&#x1D43B;<sub>&#x1D453;</sub></italic> (constructed from the DSL constraints) into a form that can be implemented with quantum operations. <sc>EnQ</sc> achieves this by using a second quantization representation internally and then converting it to Pauli matrices. In second quantization, we have operators <italic>a</italic>(<italic>j</italic>),<italic>a</italic><sup>&#x2020;</sup>(<italic>j</italic>) and number operators <italic>a</italic><sup>&#x2020;</sup>(<italic>j</italic>)<italic>a</italic>(<italic>j</italic>) that select or project onto certain basis states. These are mathematical tools to describe Hamiltonians, but real quantum gates typically work with Pauli <italic>X,Y,Z</italic> rotations.</p>
<p>The <sc>EnQ</sc> compiler provides a function <styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>toPauli</monospace></styled-content> that takes the number of qubits and an index for a particular choice, and returns a list of Pauli operations which together act as a projector onto that choice. For example, if we have 3 binary choices (requiring 2 qubits to encode 4 possible states, with one state unused), <styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>toPauli</monospace></styled-content>(2, i) might produce the tensor-product operator <italic>p</italic>(<italic>i</italic>) = <inline-formula> <italic>Z</italic><sup>b<sub>i,0</sub></sup>(0) &#x2297; <italic>Z</italic><sup>b<sub>i,1</sub></sup>(1) </inline-formula>(up to a scaling factor) <xref rid="fn1" ref-type="fn"><sup>1</sup></xref>, where (<italic>b</italic><sub><italic>i</italic>,0</sub>, b<sub><italic>i</italic>,1</sub>) is the binary representation of index <italic>i</italic>. This operator has the property that it gives +1 when the qubits are in state <italic>|b<sub>i</sub>b</italic>&#x2032;<sub><italic>i</italic></sub>&#x27E9; corresponding to that choice, and it gives 0 or &#x2212;1 for other states, effectively identifying the chosen basis state. Using such projectors, the compiler can assemble the problem Hamiltonian: for each possible outcome or each constraint pattern, it adds the appropriate Pauli-term with a coefficient equal to the desired energy. The end result is a decomposition of <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic> into a sum of weighted Pauli operators (single-qubit <italic>Z</italic>s, two-qubit <italic>Z</italic> &#x2297; <italic>Z</italic> interactions, etc., depending on the structure of the constraints). Similarly, the initial Hamiltonian <italic>H<sub>i</sub></italic> can be expressed in terms of Pauli <italic>X</italic> operators (since <italic>a</italic> + <italic>a</italic><sup>&#x2020;</sup> corresponds to <italic>X</italic> in the two-level qubit representation).</p>
<p>In the <sc>EnQ</sc> implementation, the transformation of individual choices to second quantization and Pauli operations is encoded as the <styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>toPauli</monospace></styled-content> function in <xref ref-type="fig" rid="fig4">Figure 5</xref>. The function also contains some optimizations, such as algebraic rewrite (<styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>distr</monospace></styled-content>), as well as combining Pauli terms (<styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>optimize</monospace></styled-content>). For example, <styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>distr</monospace></styled-content> performs the distribution law of tensors and linear sums. The <styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>optimize</monospace></styled-content> step first combines (<styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>combine</monospace></styled-content>) the terms having the same Pauli strings by summing their amplitudes and then removes (<styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>clean</monospace></styled-content>) terms having 0 amplitudes.</p>
</sec>
<sec id="sec5_4_2">
<label>5.4.2</label>
<title><italic> Gate Decomposition via Trotterization.</italic></title>
<p>Given <italic>H(s)</italic> expressed as a sum of basic operators, the next step is to turn the continuous evolution <inline-formula><italic>e</italic><sup>&#x2212;<italic>iH</italic>(<italic>s</italic>)&#x0394;<italic>t</italic></sup></inline-formula> into a sequence of gates. <sc>EnQ</sc> uses a Trotter-Suzuki decomposition to approximate the exponential of a sum of operators. In brief, if <italic>H = A + B</italic> (where <italic>A</italic> and <italic>B</italic> are operators that act on possibly different sets of qubits), we can approximate <inline-formula> <italic>e</italic><sup>&#x2212;<italic>i</italic>(<italic>A</italic>+<italic>B</italic>)&#x0394;<italic>t</italic></sup> &#x2248; <italic>e</italic><sup>&#x2212;<italic>iA</italic>&#x0394;<italic>t</italic></sup> <italic>e</italic><sup>&#x2212;<italic>iB</italic>&#x0394;<italic>t</italic></sup> </inline-formula> for a small time slice &#x0394;<italic>t</italic>, with the error of order <italic>O</italic>((&#x0394;<italic>t</italic>)<sup>2</sup>) By splitting the total evolution time <italic>T</italic> into <italic>m</italic> small slices (&#x0394;<italic>t = T/m</italic>) and alternating the application of <inline-formula> <italic>e</italic><sup>&#x2212;<italic>i</italic><italic>A</italic>&#x0394;<italic>t</italic></sup> </inline-formula> and <inline-formula> <italic>e</italic><sup>&#x2212;<italic>i</italic><italic>B</italic>&#x0394;<italic>t</italic></sup> </inline-formula>, the accumulated error becomes negligible for large <italic>m</italic>. This idea generalizes to more than two terms using Suzuki&#x2019;s higher-order formulas, but the principle remains: we trade a continuous time evolution for a sequence of short gate operations corresponding to each term in the Hamiltonian.</p>  
<p>In our setting, <italic>H</italic>(<italic>s</italic>) at any given small time interval is a sum of terms like <italic>X</italic> (from <italic>H<sub>i</sub></italic>) and various <italic>Z</italic>, <italic>ZZ</italic><xref rid="fn2" ref-type="fn"><sup>2</sup></xref>, etc. (from <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic>). The compiler identifies sets of terms that do not commute and interleaves their exponentials appropriately. Each basic term&#x2019;s exponential <inline-formula> <italic>e</italic><sup>&#x2212;<italic>i</italic><italic>&#x03B1;</italic><italic>P</italic>&#x0394;<italic>t</italic></sup> </inline-formula> (where <italic>P</italic> is a Pauli tensor term and <italic>&#x1D6FC;</italic> a coefficient) can be realized with a small gate gadget. For example, a term <italic>Z</italic>(<italic>j</italic>)<italic>Z</italic>(<italic>k</italic>)<xref rid="fn3" ref-type="fn"><sup>3</sup></xref> acting on qubits <italic>j</italic> and <italic>k</italic> can be implemented by a pair of CNOT gates bracketing a single-qubit Rz rotation on one qubit. A single-qubit term like <italic>X</italic>(<italic>j</italic>) is directly an Rx rotation on qubit <italic>j</italic>. The compiler uses known gate decompositions for each kind of term and parameter (many of these are built into libraries or are straightforward one-liners in Qiskit or similar frameworks). The compiler then strings these gate sequences together in the correct order for the required number of time slices. By choosing a sufficiently fine time discretization (large <italic>m</italic>), the final unitary operation  <inline-formula> <mml:math display="inline"> <mml:mrow> <mml:mi>U</mml:mi><mml:mo>&#x2248;</mml:mo><mml:msubsup> <mml:mi>&#x03A0;</mml:mi> <mml:mrow> <mml:mi>k</mml:mi><mml:mo>=</mml:mo><mml:mn>1</mml:mn> </mml:mrow> <mml:mi>m</mml:mi> </mml:msubsup> <mml:msup> <mml:mi>e</mml:mi> <mml:mrow> <mml:mo>-</mml:mo><mml:mi>i</mml:mi><mml:mi>H</mml:mi><mml:mo stretchy='false'>(</mml:mo><mml:mi>k</mml:mi><mml:mo>/</mml:mo><mml:mi>m</mml:mi><mml:mo stretchy='false'>)</mml:mo><mml:mi>&#x0394;</mml:mi><mml:mi>t</mml:mi> </mml:mrow> </mml:msup> </mml:mrow> </mml:math> </inline-formula> closely approximates the ideal adiabatic evolution from <italic>H<sub>i</sub></italic> to <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic>. In essence, we now have a full quantum circuit that starts from all qubits |0&#x27E9;, applies Hadamards to create the initial superposition, then applies a long sequence of controlled rotations and interactions gradually turning the initial superposition into (hopefully) the ground state of <italic>H<sub>f</sub></italic>, and finally ends with a measurement on each qubit.</p>
</sec>
</sec>
<sec id="sec5_5">
<label>5.5</label>
<title>Compiling to Analog Quantum Computers (D-Wave)</title>
<p>The compilation described above targets universal, gate-based quantum computers. However, <sc>EnQ</sc> can also compile to analog quantum hardware, such as D-Wave systems, which implement quantum annealing. These devices are not universal quantum computers but are well-suited for energy-constrained optimization (ECC) problems (<xref ref-type="sec" rid="sec7">Section 7</xref>). Unlike the general Hamiltonian model, Consisting of a tensor product of arbitrary Pauli operations, as in any quantum-annealing-based system, D-Wave users cannot define initial Hamiltonians, and the allowed Hamiltonian terms are restricted to single-qubit Pauli operators and two-qubit <italic>Z</italic><italic>Z</italic> interactions. The compilation from <sc>EnQ</sc> to D-Wave is thus a restricted subset of our general compilation pipeline, suitable only for certain <sc>EnQ</sc> programs. First, the program must assume the default uniform superposition as its initial state. Second, only the problem Hamiltonian is compiled; the initial Hamiltonian is assumed to be given. Third, after Trotterization, instead of generating quantum gates, we retain the sequence of Hamiltonian exponentials exp(-i<italic>H</italic><sub>1</sub>t)...exp(-i<italic>H</italic><sub>n</sub>t), where each <italic>H</italic><sub>j</sub> is a Pauli string with no nested linear sums. If any <italic>H</italic><sub>j</sub> contains terms outside the supported set <italic>X</italic>, <italic>Y</italic>, <italic>Z</italic>, <italic>Z</italic><italic>Z</italic>, compilation fails. Otherwise, each <italic>H</italic><sub>j</sub> is directly translated into a D-Wave-compatible Hamiltonian. A complete example of successful <sc>EnQ</sc> compilation to D-Wave is presented in  <xref ref-type="sec" rid="sec6_1">Section 6.1</xref>. Our strategy cannot directly deal with more complex problems like the graph coloring example. The reason is that our encoding typically uses more than two states per variable, requiring multiple qubits to represent colors. This can lead to high-order interactions (e.g., <italic>Z</italic><italic>Z</italic><italic>Z</italic><italic>Z</italic> or <italic>X</italic><italic>X</italic><italic>X</italic><italic>X</italic>), which are unsupported by D-Wave hardware. A possible approach in this case is to adopt a more involved compilation procedure based on the occupation model<xref rid="fn4" ref-type="fn"><sup>4</sup></xref> from <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib72">Lucas [2014a]</xref>, which introduces <italic>n</italic> binary variables <italic>x</italic>(j,i) per vertex <italic>x</italic>(j)&#x2014;one for each possible color. It then enforces color exclusivity via the constraint <inline-formula> <mml:math display="inline"> <mml:mrow> <mml:msup> <mml:mrow> <mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mn>1</mml:mn><mml:mo>-</mml:mo><mml:msubsup> <mml:mo>&#x2211;</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mi>i</mml:mi><mml:mo>=</mml:mo><mml:mn>0</mml:mn> </mml:mrow> <mml:mi>n</mml:mi> </mml:msubsup> <mml:mrow> <mml:mi>x</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mi>j</mml:mi><mml:mo>,</mml:mo><mml:mi>i</mml:mi> </mml:mrow> <mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> <mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> <mml:mn>2</mml:mn> </mml:msup> </mml:mrow> </mml:math> </inline-formula>. While this approach increases qubit and Hamiltonian resource requirements, it results in a problem Hamiltonian composed only of supported terms (<italic>X</italic>, <italic>Y</italic>, <italic>Z</italic>, <italic>Z</italic><italic>Z</italic>), making it suitable for D-Wave execution.</p>
</sec>
<sec id="sec5_6">
<label>5.6</label>
<title>Summary of the Workflow</title>
<fig id="f6" position="float">
<label>Fig. 6.</label>
<caption>
<title>EnQ Compilation to Quantum Computers</title>
</caption>
<graphic xlink:href="3747521_fig6.svg" orientation="portrait" position="float"/>
</fig>
<p>To recap, the compilation of an EnQ program to a quantum circuit involves the following steps (illustrated by <xref ref-type="fig" rid="f6">Figure 6</xref>):</p>
<list list-type="bullet">
<list-item><p>Encode Choices: Convert each abstract choice value into a binary/qubit representation.</p></list-item>
<list-item><p>Construct Hamiltonians: Using the encoded choices and the user&#x2019;s constraint function, construct the problem Hamiltonian <italic>H</italic><sub>f</sub> as a sum of operators. Each classical constraint evaluation (for a particular combination of variable values) is translated into a quantum term. Likewise, define the initial mixing Hamiltonian <italic>H</italic><sub>i</sub> on the same qubits (usually a sum of <italic>X</italic> terms). This step mirrors the classical constraints computation, but instead of producing numbers, it produces operators weighted by those numbers.</p></list-item>
<list-item><p>Translate to Pauli Gates: Express each operator in <italic>H</italic><sub>i</sub> and <italic>H</italic><sub>f</sub> using elementary Pauli matrices and tensor products. This yields a set of basic terms (e.g., single-qubit <italic>X</italic>(i), <italic>Z</italic>(i), or two-qubit <italic>Z</italic>(i)<italic>Z</italic>(j)) with associated coefficients.</p></list-item>
<list-item><p>Trotterization Time Evolution: Choose a schedule for evolving from <italic>H</italic><sub>i</sub> to <italic>H</italic><sub>f</sub> and break it into <italic>m</italic> small time steps. For each term from the previous step, compute the corresponding gate sequence for a slice of evolution (using rotations and CNOTs as needed). Then order these gates according to a Trotter-Suzuki sequence for each interval. This produces a circuit segment for each time slice. Repeat this sequence for the required number of <italic>m</italic> time steps to cover the full evolution.</p></list-item>
<list-item><p>Measurement and Decoding: Append measurement operations to the end of the circuit (one per qubit). Collect the measured outcome after the circuit is run on a quantum processor.</p></list-item>
</list>
<p>This entire workflow is implemented in our Haskell library in <xref ref-type="sec" rid="f_5">Figure 5</xref>. The <sc>EnQ</sc> Quantum instance&#x2019;s <styled-content style="color:#0000E6"><monospace>solve</monospace></styled-content> method coordinates these steps. For example, it calls <styled-content style="color:#0000E6">initialStates<monospace></monospace></styled-content> = <monospace>toHad</monospace>(...) to prepare the superposition, uses <styled-content style="color:#0000E6"><monospace>encodeChoices</monospace></styled-content> to map  choices to qubit operators, computes the list of constraint conditions and their energies, then uses a combination of <styled-content style="color:#0000E6"><monospace>decodeChoice, combine,</monospace></styled-content> and <styled-content style="color:#0000E6"><monospace>optimize</monospace></styled-content> to produce the final list of Hamiltonian terms, which it then passes to a backend (e.g., Qiskit) to generate and run the circuit. The entire compilation is thus fully automated: from the high-level problem description, we obtain a quantum circuit that can be executed on real hardware.</p>
<p>We used this compiler to run all the case-study programs on IBM quantum machines and simulators (see the next section for the results). The high-level <sc>EnQ</sc> code for problems like type inference, clique finding, or graph coloring is written in a functional style, and then our compiler translates it to low-level quantum instructions. Despite the complexity of quantum gate sequences, the conceptual model remains simple for the <sc>EnQ</sc> programmer: one writes the program in Haskell using familiar combinators, and <sc>EnQ</sc> handles the quantum execution under the hood. The successful experiments in the next section demonstrate that this approach not only preserves the clarity of the functional paradigm but also executes correctly on quantum hardware, solving the intended optimization problems.</p>

<sec id="sec6_1">

<p>We will now focus on the quantum equal-sum problem. We implement the equal-sum in <sc>EnQ</sc> as the program in <xref ref-type="fig" rid="f_7">Figure 7</xref>, a direct modeling of the equal-sum formula in <xref ref-type="sec" rid="sec3_1">Section 3.1</xref>. Here, we multiply out the square terms, expand the formula to a sum of the multiplication of distinct element pairs of the input list (we omit the pairs having the same elements). <label>6.2</label>
<title>Exact Cover ECC</title>
<p>In exact cover, given a collection <italic>S</italic> of subsets of a set <italic>V</italic>, we try to find a subcollection <italic>S<sup>*</sup></italic> of <italic>S</italic>, such that every element in <italic>X</italic> is contained in exactly one subset of <italic>S<sup>*</sup></italic> and <italic>S<sup>*</sup></italic> is a partition of <italic>X</italic> consisting subsets contained in <italic>S</italic>. Here, we use an alternative description based on the 3-Sat problem. Given an length <italic>n</italic> list, <italic>x<sub>1</sub>, ..., x<sub>n</sub></italic>, each choice can be <italic>x<sub>j</sub></italic> &#x2208; &#x007B;0,1&#x007D;. There are <italic>m</italic> constraints, <italic>h<sub>i</sub></italic> &#x2208; <italic>C = &#x007B;h<sub>1</sub>, ..., h<sub>m</sub>&#x007D;</italic>, as clause, each of which is based on three elements in the list, e.g., <italic>x<sub>&#x1D6FC;</sub>, x<sub>&#x1D6FD;</sub>,</italic> and <italic>x<sub>&#x1D6FE;</sub></italic>, with &#x1D6FC;, &#x1D6FD;, &#x1D6FE; &#x2208; [1, <italic>n </italic >]. The <italic>m </italic> clauses are satisfied if every one <italic>h<sub>i</sub></italic> of them satisfies <inline-formula> <mml:math display="inline"> <mml:mrow> <mml:msub> <mml:mi>x</mml:mi> <mml:mi>&#x1D6FC;</mml:mi> </mml:msub> <mml:mo>+</mml:mo><mml:msub> <mml:mi>x</mml:mi> <mml:mi>&#x1D6FD;</mml:mi> </mml:msub> <mml:mo>+</mml:mo><mml:msub> <mml:mi>x</mml:mi> <mml:mi>&#x1D6FE;</mml:mi> </mml:msub> <mml:mo>=</mml:mo><mml:mn>1</mml:mn> </mml:mrow> </mml:math> </inline-formula> and <italic>h<sub>i</sub>( x<sub>&#x1D6FC;</sub>, x<sub>&#x1D6FD; </sub>, x<sub>&#x1D6FE;</sub>) </italic>, i.e., each clause has exactly one variable equal to 1. Traditionally, the problem can be described as an Ising model formula used to calculate the minimal energy, as follows.</p>
<disp-formula><mml:math display='block'> <mml:mrow> <mml:msub> <mml:mo>&#x2211;</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mi>h</mml:mi> <mml:mo>&#x2208;</mml:mo> <mml:mi>C</mml:mi> </mml:mrow> </mml:msub> <mml:mrow> <mml:msub> <mml:mo>&#x2211;</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mi>&#x03B1;</mml:mi> <mml:mo>,</mml:mo> <mml:mi>&#x03B2;</mml:mi> <mml:mo>,</mml:mo> <mml:mi>&#x03B3;</mml:mi> <mml:mo>&#x2208;</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mo>[</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mn>1</mml:mn> <mml:mo>,</mml:mo> <mml:mi>n</mml:mi> </mml:mrow> <mml:mo>]</mml:mo> </mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> </mml:msub> <mml:mrow> <mml:msup> <mml:mrow> <mml:mrow> <mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:msub> <mml:mi>h</mml:mi> <mml:mi>i</mml:mi> </mml:msub> <mml:mrow> <mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:msub> <mml:mi>x</mml:mi> <mml:mi>&#x03B1;</mml:mi> </mml:msub> <mml:mo>,</mml:mo> <mml:msub> <mml:mi>x</mml:mi> <mml:mi>&#x03B2;</mml:mi> </mml:msub> <mml:mo>,</mml:mo> <mml:msub> <mml:mi>x</mml:mi> <mml:mi>&#x03B3;</mml:mi> </mml:msub> </mml:mrow> <mml:mo>)</mml:mo> </mml:mrow> <mml:mo>&#x2217;</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:mo>(</mml:mo> <mml:mrow> <mml:msub> <mml:mi>x</mml:mi> <mml:mi>&#x03B1;</mml:mi> </mml:msub> <mml:mo>,</mml:mo> <mml:msub> <mml:mi>x</mml:mi> <mml:mi>&#x03B2;</mml:mi> </mml:msub> <mml:mo>,</mml:mo> <mml:msub> <mml:mi>x</mml:mi> <mml:mi>&#x03B3;</mml:mi> </mml:msub> <mml:mo>&#x2212;</mml:mo> <mml:mn>1</mml:mn> </mml:mrow> <mml:mo>)</mml:mo> </mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> <mml:mo>)</mml:mo> </mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> <mml:mn>2</mml:mn> </mml:msup> </mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> </mml:mrow> </mml:math> </disp-formula>

The exact cover formalism is similar to the traditional 3-Sat solving problem, demonstrating that our <sc>EnQ</sc> system can deal with different viewpoints. The <sc>EnQ</sc> implementation is given in <xref ref-type="fig" rid="f_9">Figure 9</xref>, with the viewpoint being 3 and <styled-content style="color:#CC4747"><monospace>Cover</monospace></styled-content> being a data structure, having a list of variable location names <styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>vars</monospace></styled-content>, where the <italic>j</italic>-th element represents the <italic>j</italic>-th variable for a boolean condition (<styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>valuation</monospace></styled-content>). The <styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "> <monospace>valuation</monospace></styled-content> field stores a function taking in a three-variable satisfiability function with three choices, answering a Boolean value. In our <styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "> <monospace>constraints</monospace></styled-content> implementation, we check the satisfiability of the three variables <styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>a</monospace></styled-content>, <styled-content style="color:#0000E6
 "><monospace>b</monospace></styled-content>, and <styled-content style="color:#0000E6

<sec id="sec7">
<label>7</label>
<title>Related Work</title>
<p><sc>EnQ</sc> is a unified programming library for programmers to program ECC problems in the classical NP and P classes and compile them to be AQC procedures in quantum circuits. We provide related work studies for ECC and AQC problems here.</p>
<p><italic><bold>AQC and ECC.</bold></italic> The concept of quantum computation based on the adiabatic procedure as proposed by <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib38">Farhi et al. [2000]</xref>, and related ideas were suggested by <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib60">Kadowaki and Nishimori [1998]</xref> and <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">Brooke et al. [1999]</xref>. A closely related concept is quantum annealing, a physical process that attempts to implement algorithms for AQC, first proposed by <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib20">Chakrabarti [1981]</xref>, with many subsequence developments [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">Brooke et al. 1999</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib40">Finnila et al. 1994</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib60">Kadowaki and Nishimori 1998</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib83">Ray et al. 1989</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib100">Wu et al. 1991</xref>]. It incorporates algorithms and hardware designed to solve computational problems via quantum evolution towards the ground states of final Hamiltonians that encode classical optimization problems, without necessarily insisting on universality or adiabaticity.</p>
<p>Many of the NP-complete problems are framed in terms of the Ising model [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Barahona 1982</xref>], a physical model, a mathematical model of ferromagnetism in statistical mechanics. The idea is to think of NP-complete problems as ECC problems, with mathematical formalism in the Ising model, and solve them in AQC or quantum annealing [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib28">Das and Chakrabarti 2008;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib37">Farhi et al. 2001</xref>]. Many previous AQC-related works have been proposed to solve NP-complete problems [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Alon et al. 1998;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Childs et al. 2002;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">Choi 2010;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib43">Fu and Anderson 1986;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib61">Karp 1972</xref>] by framing them as Ising models. Lucas [2014b] provides a good overview of framing different NP-problems in the Ising model. Our clique finding algorithm is more similar to the algorithm in <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib99">Wu [1982]</xref>. Note that the Ising model framed ECC problems often go by the name QUBO (quadratic unconstrained binary optimization) in many mathematical literature [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Boros</xref> and <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">Hammer 1991, 2002]</xref>. QUBO is a mathematical formalism for solving combinatorial optimization problems [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib63">Kochenberger et al. 2014</xref>]. QUBO is closely related to Ising model-based ECC problems. D-wave [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib52">Inc 2025</xref>] is a quantum computer provider, mainly focusing on utilizing quantum annealing to solve ECC problems with Ising model-based Hamiltonian programs.</p>
<p>Compared to prior work, <sc>EnQ</sc> generalizes the front-end problem modeling beyond the <italic>QUBO</italic> or Ising restrictions typically imposed by D-Wave-style architectures: users can specify arbitrary constraint structures (such as type inference) without manually encoding them into spin models. Manytimes, the <sc>EnQ</sc> generalization provides a more effective way of compiling an Ising problem. The D-Wave description for graph coloring requires <italic>n</italic> different binary variables to represent <italic>n</italic> different colors, each variable states the on-and-off of a color, which leads to an additional problem constraint term to enforce the color uniqueness in a vertex [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib72">Lucas 2014a</xref>], while our modeling enumerates colors and embeds colors as different numbers, representable as qubit states; thus, no need to have the extra uniqueness constraint. The <sc>EnQ</sc> current backend compilation targets circuit-based <italic>AQC</italic> simulation (for example, via <italic>Qiskit</italic> Trotterization), because D-Wave is not a universal quantum anealer, e.g., the clique finding example requires a special initial superposition state that is not supported in D-Wave.</p>  
<p><italic><bold>Hamiltonian Simulation based Quantum Computation and Second Quantization</bold></italic>. There are a few quantum computation prototypes allowing users to define Hamiltonians based on Pauli groups (X, Y, and Z), then they are able to compile the simulation of the Hamiltonians with respect to time, to quantum computers. These works include IBM Qiskit Nature [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib26">Cross et al. 2022</xref>], QuEra Bloqade [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib1">blo 2023</xref>], OpenFermion [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib76">McClean et al. 2019</xref>], InQuanto[<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib52">Inc. 2025</xref>], SimuQ [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib80">Peng et al. 2024</xref>], and Pasqal Pulser [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib91">Silv&#x00E9;rio et al. 2022</xref>] developed by hardware service providers. QuTip [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib56">Johansson et al. 2012</xref>], supporting Hamiltonian simulation in a classical computer. These works are inspired by many classical analog compilations [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Achour and Rinard 2020; Achour et al. 2016</xref>].</p>
<p>Most of these works are developed to support scientific computation in physics, chemistry, and biology.</p>
<p>Second quantization, or occupation number representation, is a formalism used to describe and analyze quantum particle systems in scientific computation. The key ideas of the methodology were introduced by Paul Dirac [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib31">Dirac and Bohr 1927</xref>] and were later developed by Pascual Jordan [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib58">Jordan and Wigner 1928</xref>] and Vladimir Fock [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib41">Fock 1932</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib84">Reed and Simon 1975</xref>]. In this approach, the quantum states are represented in the Fock state basis, which is constructed by filling up each single-particle state with a certain number of identical particles [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">Becchi 2010</xref>]. Later, many works were developed for describing second quantization in analyzing different systems, such as fermions in lattice-based systems [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib66">Levin and Wen 2003</xref>], chemical and biological molecule systems [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib44">Gori et al. 2023</xref>], nuclear physical systems [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib24">Christiansen 2004</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib57">Johnson et al. 2013</xref>]. Recently, researchers developed algebraic formalisms based on second quantization and Lie algebra [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib11">Batista and Ortiz 2004;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib87">Schwarz 2021</xref>] as a new extension of second quantization, in order to define the transformations from one particle system to another, such as defining a generalization of Jordan Wigner Transformation [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib10">Batista and Ortiz 2001</xref>] to transform different particle systems to Pauli group based Hamiltonians.</p>
<p><italic><bold>Quantum Circuit-based Programming Languages</bold></italic>. Quantum circuit-based language developments are focal. Q# [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib94">Svore et al. 2018</xref>], Quilc [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib93">Smith et al. 2020</xref>], ScaffCC [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib55">JavadiAbhari et al. 2015</xref>], Project Q [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib93">Steiger et al. 2018</xref>], Criq [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib29">Developers 2023</xref>], Qiskit [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib6">Aleksandrowicz et al. 2019</xref>] are industrial quantum circuit languages, without formal semantics, but based on a standardized denotational quantum circuit semantics. There are formally verifying quantum circuit programs, including Qwire [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib81">Rand 2018</xref>], SQIR [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib50">Hietala et al. 2021</xref>], and QBricks [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib21">Chareton et al. 2021</xref>], quantum Hoare logic and its subsequent works [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib71">Liu et al. 2019</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib102">Ying 2012</xref>; <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib105">Zhou et al. 2023</xref>], Qafny [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib69">Li et al. 2024</xref>]. These tools have been used to verify a range of quantum algorithms, from Grover&#x2019;s search to quantum phase estimation. There are works verifying quantum circuit optimizations (e.g., <sc>voqc</sc> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib51">Hietala et al. 2023</xref>], CertiQ [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib89">Shi et al. 2019</xref>]), as well as verifying quantum circuit compilation procedures, including ReVerC [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib8">Amy et al. 2017</xref>] and ReQWIRE [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib82">Rand et al. 2018</xref>].</p>
<p>There are functional language interpretations for quantum circuits [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib33">D&#x00ED;az-Caro et al. 2019;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib42">Fu et al. 2023;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib45">Grattage 2005</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib46">2011</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib47">2006;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib48">Green et al. 2013;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib86">Sabry et al. 2018;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib88">Selinger and Valiron 2013;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib95">van Tonder 2004;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib96">Vizzotto et al. 2013;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib97">Voichick et al. 2023;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib103">Ying 2016</xref>], with formal semantics. These languages are mainly designed to encode quantum circuits as functional language constructs, such as quantum pattern matching. Silq [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Bichsel et al. 2020</xref>] proposed a language to perform automatic uncomputation with denotational semantics for describing circuit behaviors. ZX-calculus [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Coecke and Duncan 2011;</xref> <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="bib98">Wang 2023</xref>] turns elements appearing quantum semantics in a diagram calculus for expressing unitary operations without measurement.</p>
</sec>
<sec id="sec8">
<label>8</label>
<title>Conclusion</title>
<p>We present <sc>EnQ</sc>, a system for describing ECC problems. <sc>EnQ</sc> intends to lift the ECC problem descriptions from low-level bit ones to high-level descriptions based on the problems users intend to solve. We demonstrate NP and P problems that can be thought of as ECC problems, solvable in <sc>EnQ</sc>, by embedding the problems to a classical interpreter and quantum circuits. <sc>EnQ</sc> also provides a programming platform for AQC, a long-existing quantum computation model lacking recognition due to its unintuitive programming interface, i.e., it is hard to think of problems to be programmed in AQC. In <sc>EnQ</sc>, describing an NP or P problem has no difference in terms of the classical and quantum interpretations, as we marry the two worlds to provide users with a uniform interface. We also demonstrate the classical interpretation and end-to-end quantum compilation for an ECC program in <sc>EnQ</sc>. The quantum compilation connects AQC with traditional circuit-based quantum compilation.</p>
<p>For future work, we intend to develop verification and testing toolchains based on <sc>EnQ</sc>, as well as imposing different programming language mechanisms to help the analysis of <sc>EnQ</sc> programs.</p>
